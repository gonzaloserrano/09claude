## Topic

An extension of Matt Pocock’s “grill me” skill pattern toward domain-driven design, pushing the skill from generic questioning into a more specific architectural lens.

## Research Summary

- Matt Pocock’s skills repository and the existing Grill Me skill confirm the base pattern is real and already popular.
- The new “ddd” variant is not broadly documented yet, but it fits the repo’s philosophy of turning reusable prompting patterns into composable skills.
- The important idea is specialization: a good skill becomes more valuable when it grills a design from a specific perspective instead of asking generic questions.

## Key Findings

- Skill variants let one interaction pattern serve multiple design lenses without reinventing the whole workflow.
- Domain-driven design is a good fit for “grill me” because it depends on surfacing boundaries, language, and invariants early.
- Popularity matters here because backwards compatibility and naming become real concerns once a skill gets widely reused.

## Practical Relevance (for Gonzalo)

- Useful if Gonzalo wants reusable design interrogation patterns instead of ad hoc architecture chats.
- The broader lesson is to specialize skills around decision frameworks, not just output formats.

## Risks / Caveats

- Highly specific skill variants can fragment naming and make discovery harder.
- A DDD framing can be overkill on small or highly pragmatic projects.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. Keep a base questioning skill plus specialized variants for architecture, DDD, or risk review.
2. Use DDD-flavored grilling only when boundaries and language actually matter.
3. Prefer evolvable naming so popular skills do not become hard to maintain.
