## Topic

An early practitioner reaction that Opus 4.7 feels more intelligent and agentic than 4.6, but also changes cost dynamics and requires adaptation.

## Research Summary

- Anthropic is explicitly positioning Opus 4.7 as a capability upgrade, and external write-ups reinforce that the model is stronger on one-shot and agentic tasks.
- At the same time, user replies about token cost and changed behavior are credible reminders that capability gains are not free.
- The most balanced reading is that 4.7 raises the ceiling but also makes workflow hygiene and spend awareness more important.

## Key Findings

- A model can be more capable and still feel worse if the user’s workflow assumes older defaults.
- Adaptive reasoning and longer autonomous behavior shift how usage limits are felt in practice.
- Early release sentiment often splits between users who adapt quickly and users who hit new failure modes first.

## Practical Relevance (for Gonzalo)

- Relevant if Gonzalo wants the right mental model for adopting a stronger but more opinionated frontier model.
- This is a good reminder to separate capability from ergonomics and cost.

## Risks / Caveats

- Early impressions can overweight novelty, token-limit pain, or one unusually good session.
- A stronger model can still regress on specific workflows users care about.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. Evaluate Opus 4.7 on a fixed set of representative tasks before drawing conclusions.
2. Track cost, completion quality, and supervision load together.
3. Update prompts and verification steps if the model behaves more autonomously than before.
