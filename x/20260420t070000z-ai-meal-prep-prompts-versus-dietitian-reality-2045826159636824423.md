## Topic

AI can help structure meal-planning workflows, but current outputs can drift meaningfully from dietitian-grade nutrition plans.

## Research Summary

- The post's usefulness claim is credible for brainstorming, grocery organization, and rough meal structure.
- The stronger medical or precision-nutrition claim is much weaker, and the cited Frontiers study supports that caution.
- Official consumer guidance from Mayo Clinic and USDA is still a better anchor for healthy meal planning than prompt cleverness alone.

## Key Findings

- The Frontiers abstract reports clinically significant deviations between AI diet plans and dietitian reference plans.
- That makes AI a reasonable planning assistant, but an unreliable substitute where nutritional precision matters.
- Healthy meal structure is still better grounded in established guidance like Mayo Clinic and USDA MyPlate resources.

## Practical Relevance (for Gonzalo)

- Useful if Gonzalo wants AI help turning constraints into shopping lists or weekly meal structures.
- Less suitable if the goal is medically meaningful personalization or accurate macro planning without oversight.

## Risks / Caveats

- Nutritional mistakes can matter more than typical productivity-tool mistakes.
- Consumer AI outputs may sound confident even when quantities or dietary assumptions are off.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. Use AI for brainstorming and organization, not for unsupervised nutrition precision.
2. Cross-check any serious meal plan against established dietary guidance.
3. Keep dietitian input in the loop when health conditions or strict targets matter.
